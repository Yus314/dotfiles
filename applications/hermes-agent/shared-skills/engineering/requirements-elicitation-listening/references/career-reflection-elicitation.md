# Career Reflection Elicitation

Use this mode when the user wants to deepen career self-understanding rather than
receive generic advice.

## Dialogue Pattern

1. Ask one question at a time and choose the next question from the answer.
2. Begin with lived sources of energy: what feels interesting, meaningful, useful,
   recognized, or painful.
3. Reflect a small increment back before continuing. Sharpen hypotheses without
   closing the inquiry.
4. Distinguish values, preferred work objects, cognitive style, environment needs,
   and risk signals.
5. Periodically state what has become clearer and whether the current questions are
   advancing the user's goal.

## Useful Axes

- broad impact versus direct user value;
- foundational technology versus product work;
- new principles or reframing versus refinement;
- elegant/simple ideas versus complex/powerful systems;
- solo deep work versus collaborative discussion;
- initial scaffolding versus long-term autonomy;
- technical depth, recognition, usefulness, and growth.

These axes are prompts, not a personality taxonomy. Add, remove, or rename them in
the user's own language.

## Example Question Sequence

1. When do you feel most interested or energized in work?
2. What does “useful” mean to you?
3. What kind of impact do you want your work to have?
4. Do you prefer discovering a new way to see a problem or refining an established
   approach?
5. How do ideas form best for you: writing, diagrams, experiments, conversation, or
   another process?
6. What kind of listener or reviewer helps unfinished ideas develop?
7. What work conditions feel most painful?
8. What support and onboarding would help you grow without taking away ownership?
9. What would meaningful progress over the next few years look like?

## Pitfalls

- Do not jump directly to a job title.
- Do not batch the questions when one-at-a-time dialogue was requested.
- Do not mistake a request for guidance for lack of self-direction.
- Keep personal conclusions in the relevant profile's notes or memory, not in this
  shared procedural reference.
